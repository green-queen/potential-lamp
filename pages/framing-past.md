# Framing the Past: Photography and Narratives of Palestine (1936-1948)
[image]

## Statement of Pedagogical Intent
The goal of this cluster’s materials is to encourage students to examine how photography from the period of 1936–1948 can produce different narratives about the same events. This era, marking a time of increased unrest under British colonial rule, offers an interesting collection of photographic media promoting different points of view. This cluster highlights how access to these images shapes modern perceptions of the conflicts in Palestine. By exploring both primary sources, including photos of everyday life, landscapes, and official documentation, and secondary sources that critically analyze photography and archives, this cluster asks students to see photographic materials in a new light: not just as neutral records of history but as constructed representations spotlighting particular perspectives and shaping different understandings of  what happened during the time period. Importantly, these sources were not widely accessible at the time of their production because of limited technology, lack of internet access, and the absence of curated collections like the ones shown. This cluster invites students engaging with the material to recognize the power of media narratives and to notice how their preservation and reinterpretation in the present shape how contemporary audiences understand and remember this particular historical period.

## Discussion Questions
1. To what extent can photography be considered an objective record of history? How do choices in photography, like framing, angle, and captioning, shape the meaning of a photo?  
2. What roles do media producer, authorities, and institutions play in shaping narratives and inserting their perspective? How do they influence which narratives become dominant or marginalized?  
3. How does access to these sources shift what people know about this time period? How might modern use of these sources shape their meaning and purpose?

## In-class activity
Divide students into small groups to look at different photos from the time period of 1936-1948. Students will identify and discuss information such as who is the author/creator, who is the audience, what message is the media trying to convey, what perspectives are emphasized or marginalized, and what each source makes them feel as an audience. Each group can then share out to the class for broader discussion.
