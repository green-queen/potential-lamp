#  The Creation of Queer Self-Identity Under Settler Colonialism
[image]

## Statement of Pedagogical Intent
This cluster creates a comprehensive picture of the nuanced impacts of settler colonialism on queer self-identification and broader queer identity. It is comprised of three materials: an academic paper studying the possible intersections between queer theory, indigenous studies, and theories of settler colonialism; a book (of which the first chapter is suggested as an in-class reading) including queer Palestinians’ descriptions of their lives facing Zionist oppression and internal homophobia; and an Instagram account run by queer Gazawi who opine on queer and decolonial Palestinian identity. These sources introduce a variety of perspectives, from the academic and theoretical to first-person reports from Palestine, around what author Sa’ad Atshan calls the “empire of critique”: the overlapping intersections of homophobia and settler colonialism, both internal and external, that stand as an obstacle to liberation for queer Palestinians. 

This particular group of materials communicates a diverse set of experiences faced by colonized queer and transgender people, with a historical and academic view of the ideologies and historical trends that led to their situations and subsequent identity formation processes. 
Students engaging with these materials, which are deliberately (meta)critical and diverse in their methods of communication, will learn to understand the agency and identities of queer indigenous peoples, and comprehend their criticisms of political realities and the historical situations underlying them. They also convey an intersectional view of queer indigenous identity as both a pair of literal identities and positionalities, avoiding the risk that Andrea Smith describes in her paper that, when "indigeneity is not foregrounded, it tends to disappear in order to enable the emergence of the hybrid subject" (51).


## Discussion Questions
1. The three sources study queerness from the perspective of Native American and Palestinian identities. What do the authors critique within their respective societies, queer activism, and indigenous studies/activism? How are these critiques different or similar between the Smith piece and the Atshan/Ward sources?
2. Do you agree with Smith's measured endorsement of subjectless critique within queer theory, wherein she argues that subjectless critique can show that “Native studies is an intellectual project that has broad applicability not only for Native peoples but for everyone” while also running the risk of retrenching “white, middle-class identity”? Is the concept of subjectless critique interfaced with, whether or not by name, within the other sources; if so, what do the sources appear to believe about the value of the concept?
3. Many of these sources critique both the processes of colonization that imported Western gender norms and current systems of existence, which aren't often tied to colonial governments explicitly (and in some cases claim to oppose colonial powers) but that still reify those norms. How can colonized people both reclaim their relationship to the land and to their queer identities, and is it possible to transform colonized peoples’ governments and liberation movements to uplift all oppressed people?
4. The Ward Instagram platform presents critiques of Palestinian civil society and governance, Israel’s pinkwashing campaign and genocidal violence against Palestinians, and global Palestine solidarity activists’ tendency to overlook or sacrifice the voices of queer Palestinians for a broader “anti-colonial movement”. How does the Ward Instagram platform present decolonial queer Palestinian identity amidst all of these critiques? When considered as a platform meant to message to Palestine activists outside of Gaza, what goals is the platform trying to achieve?
