# Practicing Care and Ethics in the Midst of War and Genocide
[image]

## Statement of Pedagogical Intent
The goal of bringing these materials together is to allow students to engage in thoughtful ethical analyses and confront how ethical, political, and structural knowledge of medical care shift in contexts of war and genocide. By pairing real-world dilemmas with theoretical questions about medical responsibility, these sources will make students question how violence reshapes the ethical burden and moral responsibilities of health workers; challenge the principles of humanitarian ethics; and expose the limits of existing ethical frameworks that can’t exist within ongoing violence. This set of materials is meant to provoke emotional discomfort, critical reflection, and a deep understanding of how medical ethics might collapse or transform in a space where health systems are being targeted deliberately. Students engaging with these materials will evaluate available choices and consider how international stakeholders intervene (or interfere) to alleviate circumstances under which medicine can be practiced.


## Discussion Questions
1. Where do the principles of humanitarian medical ethics fail health workers operating inside active genocide?  
2. When health workers are consistently forced to choose ethically “wrong” treatment options (according to traditional medical ethics guidance)—such as treating patients without anesthesia or exposing civilians to danger—how do we understand moral responsibility?


## In-class activity
Pose this ethical dilemma to students: A major hospital has been partially destroyed during a military assault. Power is intermittent, water is contaminated, and thousands of citizens have taken shelter inside of what’s left of the hospital unit. As one of the few remaining medical professionals, you are asked your input on the following crisis. There has been an increase in respiratory infections and all of them have been traced to the area where the hospital is located. Do you let people stay in shelter and risk a further outbreak that could kill your patients? If not, what alternatives do you have?
What ethical concepts are at play in this case?
